Web pioneers

Vannevar Bush, D.Eng.


Vannevar Bush, (born March 11, 1890, Everett, Mass., U.S.—died June 28, 1974, Belmont, Mass.), American electrical engineer and administrator who developed the Differential Analyzer and oversaw government mobilization of scientific research during World War II.[1]

He was a key figure in the development of electrical, computer, and electronic technologies. He built the country's electric power grid, and soon electronic gadgets were everywhere. In some ways, he is credited with inventing hypertext and the World Wide Web.[1]

Doug Englebart, PhD


Douglas Engelbart, (born January 30, 1925, Portland, Oregon, U.S.—died July 2, 2013, Atherton, California), American inventor whose work beginning in the 1950s led to his patent for the computer mouse, the development of the basic graphical user interface (GUI), and groupware.[2]

Engelbart's vision was to utilize computers to connect people in a network so they could exchange and update information in "real time." He coupled this concept of collaborative software, or groupware. In 1963, SRI partially sponsored Engelbart's own research laboratory, the Augmentation Research Center, where he worked on designing and improving different data input, manipulation, and display methods, such as the computer mouse, multiple-window display, and hypermedia (the linking of texts, images, video, and sound files within a single document).[2]

Ted Nelson, PhD


Theodor Holm Nelson (born June 17, 1937) is an American pioneer of information technology, philosopher, and sociologist. He coined the terms hypertext and hypermedia in 1963. and published them in 1965. Nelson coined the terms transclusion, virtuality, and intertwingularity (in Literary Machines).[3]

The closest modern parallel would be to create a list of bookmarks to thematically relevant Web pages and then let the user to browse through the list forward and backward.[3]

Steve Jobs


Steve Jobs, in full Steven Paul Jobs, (born February 24, 1955, San Francisco, California, U.S.—died October 5, 2011, Palo Alto, California), cofounder of Apple Computer, Inc. (now Apple Inc.), and a charismatic pioneer of the personal computer era.[4]

When Jobs was only 21, he invented the first Apple computer, which transformed the public's perception of a computer from a massive equipment for scientific purpose to a household appliance that anybody could use. Apple's Macintosh computer, released in 1984, offered a graphical user interface and mouse technology that set the standard for all application interfaces. Apple became a public business in 1980, and Jobs was named chairman. The eye-catching new iMac computer (1998) turned around the company's fortunes. Apple became an industry leader and one of the most valuable firms in the world under Jobs' direction. Its other major products include iTunes (2001), the iPod (2001), the iPhone (2007), and the iPad (2010).[4]

Steve Wozniak


Stephen G. Wozniak (born August 11, 1950 in San Jose, California, USA) is a computer engineer from the United States. While still in his teens, he invented electrical devices and games. He was a Hewlett-Packard employee in the 1970s. He and Steven Jobs co-founded Apple Computer in 1976. (incorporated in 1977; now Apple Inc.). He took a leave of absence from Apple after being seriously injured in an aircraft crash in 1981, but he returned to work on the groundbreaking Macintosh computer. He departed Apple permanently in 1985, the year he was given the National Medal of Technology. Since then, he has taught in primary schools.[5]

Tim Berners-Lee, KBE


Tim Berners-Lee, in full Sir Tim Berners-Lee, (born June 8, 1955, London, England), British computer scientist, generally credited as the inventor of the World Wide Web. In 2004 he was awarded a knighthood by Queen Elizabeth II of the United Kingdom and the inaugural Millennium Technology Prize (€1 million) by the Finnish Technology Award Foundation.[6]

While at CERN, Berners-Lee created a software called Enquire for himself that could store information in files with connections ("links") both within and between independent files—a technology that became known as hypertext. In Ferndown, Dorset, where he built a range of computer systems. He returned to CERN in 1984 to work on the construction of the laboratory's computer network, creating methods that allowed various computers to interact with one another and researchers to operate faraway devices. Berners-Lee proposed a worldwide hypertext document system based on the Internet in 1989.[6]

Marc Andreessen


Marc Andreessen, (born July 9, 1971, Cedar Falls, Iowa, U.S.), American-born software engineer who played a key role in creating the Web browser Mosaic and who cofounded Netscape Communications Corporation.[7]

James Clark was looking for an interesting new enterprise, and he found it with Andreessen. Mosaic Communications Corporation was established in April 1994 by the pair (later rechristened Netscape Communications). Andreessen hired the original Mosaic brains and set out to construct the "monster" software, which they first named Mozilla (meaning Mosaic Killer). It was commercially released as Netscape Navigator and quickly became the most popular Web browser, capturing over 75% of the market share by mid-1996.[7]

Eric Bina


Eric J. Bina (born October 1964) is an American software programmer who is the co-creator of Mosaic and the co-founder of Netscape. In 1993, Bina along with Marc Andreessen authored the first version of Mosaic while working as a programmer at National Center for Supercomputing Applications (NCSA) at the University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign.[8]

Bina and Andreessen began working on Mosaic in December 1992, and by March 1993, they had a functioning prototype. Mosaic was the first killer program that popularized the Internet once it was placed on the Internet. He is one of the five first inductees into the World Wide Web Hall of Fame, which was unveiled during the first international conference on the World Wide Web in 1994. Bina and Andreessen were honored with the ACM Software System Award in 1995.[8]

Rob Hartill, PhD


Robert Hartill (born 30 January 1969 in Pontypridd, Wales) is a computer programmer and web designer best known for his work on the Internet Movie Database website and the Apache web server and is notable for playing a key role in the initial growth of the World Wide Web. [9]

He launched the initial online version of the database on August 5, 1993. Los Alamos National Laboratory published an e-print collection on ArXiv.org with Paul Ginsparg in 1994. He was also a co-founder of the Apache Software Foundation and contributed significantly to the early development of the Apache HTTP Server. Hartill was one of just six people named to the World Wide Web Hall of Fame in 1994, at the inaugural international World Wide Web conference. Rob returned to Ogmore-by-Sea in Wales when the Internet Movie Database was formed in 1996, before quitting the IMDb in 2000 and moving to South Africa.[9]

Lou Montulli


Louis J. Montulli II (best known as Lou Montulli) is a computer programmer who is well known for his work in producing web browsers. In 1991 and 1992, he co-authored a text web browser called Lynx, with Michael Grobe and Charles Rezac, while he was at the University of Kansas. This web browser was one of the first available and is still in use today.[10]

He wrote the networking technology for the earliest versions of the Netscape web browser. He was also responsible for a number of browser developments, including HTTP cookies, the blink element, server push and client pull, HTTP proxying, and advocating the incorporation of animated GIFs into browsers. He was also a founding member of the W3C's HTML working group and a contributing author of the HTML 3.2 definition while at Netscape. He is one of only six honorees into the World Wide Web Hall of Fame, which was unveiled during the First International Conference on the World Wide Web in 1994.[10]

[22] Lou Montulli

Bob Kahn


Robert Elliot Kahn (born December 23, 1938) is an American electrical engineer, who, along with Vint Cerf, first proposed the Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) and the Internet Protocol (IP), the fundamental communication protocols at the heart of the Internet.[11]

While working on the SATNET satellite packet network project, he came up with the initial ideas for what later became the Transmission Control Protocol (TCP), which was intended as a replacement for an earlier network protocol, NCP, used in the ARPANET. TCP played a major role in forming the basis of open-architecture networking, which would allow computers and networks all over the world to communicate with each other, regardless of what hardware or software the computers on each network used.[11]

Vint Cerf, PhD


Vinton Cerf, in full Vinton Gray Cerf, (born June 23, 1943, New Haven, Connecticut, U.S.), American computer scientist who is considered one of the founders, along with Robert Kahn, of the Internet.”[12]

Cerf worked in the laboratory of Leonard Kleinrock at UCLA under fellow student Stephen Crocker on the effort to create the communication protocol (Network Control Program [or Protocol]; NCP) for the ARPANET (Advanced Research Projects Agency Network; see DARPA), the first computer network based on packet switching, an unproven technology at the time. (Unlike traditional telephone communications, which require a dedicated circuit for transmission, packet switching divides a message into "packets" that travel independently over many circuits.) UCLA was one of the four initial ARPANET nodes. Cerf was also involved in the development of software that evaluated and assessed the ARPANET's performance.[12]
